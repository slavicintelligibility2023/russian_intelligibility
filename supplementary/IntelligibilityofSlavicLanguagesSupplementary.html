  1. Overall Evaluation of Results

Fig. Sup_7. Distribution languages among Slavic languages,
data are taken from https://glottolog.org/resource/languoid/id/slav1255 

All answers were evaluated according to the following scale by two assessors.

  • A word from the original text or its close synonym were considered as a “completely correct” answer. Such answers were assigned 1 point. In the example below such words as замкнуться ‘withdraw into oneself’, отгородиться ‘shut oneself off’ were considered completely correct

Поэтому Иван и от этого пути отказался, решив избрать третий: _(замкнуться)_ в гордом молчании.

…so Ivan rejected that course too, deciding to adopt the third: he would__( wrap himself)______ in proud silence.

  • Words with the same root but of a different part of speech and other words of the same part of speech which did not break the narrative (i.e. words which did not render the phrase ungrammatical or make it lose sense, but the author was talking about something different there) were considered as “partially correct”, e.g., for the given example such verbs as сидеть ‘sit’, замереть ‘freeze’, ждать ‘wait’ were considered “partially correct”. In this case we believed that the participant was close in their guess, but did not quite get the sense of the omitted word. Such answers were assigned 0.5 points.
  • A blank field, an incorrect part of speech which renders the phrase ungrammatical, and words which break the narrative (i.e. make it lose logical sense) were considered as incorrect. For example, одиночество ‘loneliness’ was incorrect in the example above. Such answers received 0 points.

We calculated the average correctness of participants’ answers in tests (as described above). Fig. 1 and Table 2 demonstrate the distribution of calculated average scores according to the language of parallel text together with the control group results. Every point represents the intelligibility of one test for a participant, a cross represents the average score among participants for the parallel language, the bordered vertical line in each distribution demonstrates the 95% confidence interval, a star represents the median value for the parallel language, the grey horizontal bar demonstrates the confidence interval for the control group; the numbers next to crosses show the difference between the average score for control group tests and the average score for this particular language tests.

  1. Intelligibility of Word Categories

To do it we classified all pairs of omitted words and their equivalents in our experiment into the following four groups.

  • The first category consisted of such words from parallel texts which were perceived as stemming from the same root as the original omitted word[1] (referred to as ‘same root’ below).
  • The second category consisted of parallel text words which were perceived as stemming from the same root as synonyms or semantically close words to the omitted one (‘cognate synonyms’).
  • The third category consisted of non-cognates, i.e. totally unrelated words having no written or phonetic correspondence with the omitted one (Frunza, 2006) (‘non-cognates’).
  • The fourth category consisted of false friends to the omitted Russian word, the ones that are perceived as similar but have different translations (Frunza, 2006) (‘false friends’).

Fig. 3 shows the difference between the intelligibility of an individual word in a control group and in a test with a parallel language (as grouped by tests). One point here means average intelligibility of an individual word (i.e. how well a Russian word was restored in a control group test or in a test with a parallel text in a given foreign language averaged by occurrences in this test and by all participants for this language). Coloured crosses represent the average and coloured lines show 95% confidence interval for tests with parallel language texts (results are grouped by tests), red crosses and lines correspond to the same figures for control group tests.

Fig. 3. Intelligibility of words in accordance with their class and test

A point in Fig. Sup_8 corresponds to the relation between the intelligibility of a word in a control group test and in a test with a given parallel language. The colour and the marker of a point correspond to the class of the word’s equivalent in a parallel text.

The general trends being as they are described above, the relation between the intelligibility of a test and the percentage of words of different classes in it is not as straightforward as it may seem. An increase in the number of same root words does not necessarily lead to an increase in the intelligibility of the whole test. Similarly, an increase in the number of non-cognates does not always lead to a decrease in the test intelligibility.
